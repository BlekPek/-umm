Modern Era of Cars

The modern era is usually defined as the 25 years proceeding the current year. However, there are some technical and design aspects that differentiate modern cars from antiques. The modern era has been one of increasing standardization, platform sharing, and computer-aided design (1). Some particular contemporary developments (2) are the proliferation of front-and all-wheel drive (4), the adoption of the diesel engine, and the ubiquity (5) of fuel injection. Most modern passenger cars are front-wheel-drive unibody designs.

Cars are equipped with controls used for driving, parking, passenger comfort and safety, and controlling a variety of lights. Over the decades, additional features and controls have been added to vehicles, making them progressively more complex. Examples include rear reversing cameras (6), air conditioning, navigation systems, and in car entertainment.

Most cars in use in the 2010s are propelled by an internal combustion engine, fueled gasoline (also known as petrol) or diesel. Both fuels cause air pollution and are also blamed for contributing to climate change and global warming. Vehicles using alternative fuels such as ethanol flexible-fuel vehicles (7) and natural gas vehicles are also gaining popularity in some countries. Electric cars, which were invented early in the history of the car, began to become commercially available (8) in 2008. Body styles (9) have changed as well in the modern era. Three types, the hatchback (10), sedan, and sport utility vehicle (SUV) (11), as well as today's high-powered luxury crossover (12), sports wagon, two-volume large MPV (13), dominate today's market.

The modern era has also seen rapidly rising (3) fuel efficiency (14) and engine output (15). The automobile emissions concerns have been eased with computerized engine management systems (16).

In the past, automotive engineers were closely associated with the field of mechanical engineering. The reality of today is that the automotive engineer must know far more than just mechanical engineering. The modem automobile is described as a computer on wheels.

Electronics control component systems such as the engine, transmission, and brakes. Those controls have become integral parts of the operation of each system and the whole vehicle. a focus is on intelligent vehicle technology, which highlighted the integration of more electronics into the vehicle. Competitive quality and cost require that the design specifications (17) match the manufacturing capability (18).
